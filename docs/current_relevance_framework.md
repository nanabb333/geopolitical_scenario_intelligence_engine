# Current Relevance Framework

The Current Relevance Framework explains how analysts can compare current events to historical analogues without forecasting.

It is a disciplined interpretation layer: it helps analysts decide whether a current question is comparable to prior events, what evidence exists, and what caveats should be included.

## Historical Similarity

Historical similarity asks whether the current question resembles coded past events.

Review:

- event family;
- event mechanism;
- geography;
- sector;
- actor and target;
- event timing and implementation status;
- source confidence.

Historical similarity does not imply that the current event will produce the same outcome.

## Structural Similarity

Structural similarity asks whether the current event has the same underlying structure as past cases.

Examples:

- restriction versus support;
- named-company action versus sector-wide rule;
- announcement versus enforcement;
- direct firm impact versus broad risk sentiment;
- policy framework versus final implementation.

Structural similarity helps prevent superficial comparisons.

## Policy Similarity

Policy similarity asks whether the policy mechanism matches prior events.

Relevant dimensions:

- export control;
- entity-list action;
- procurement restriction;
- subsidy;
- strategic investment screening;
- critical-mineral restriction;
- supply-chain relocation support.

Policy similarity is strongest when both mechanism and implementation status are comparable.

## Sector Exposure

Sector exposure asks whether the same sectors or assets are plausibly affected.

Review:

- primary sector;
- secondary sectors;
- named companies;
- proxy assets;
- benchmark choice;
- direct versus indirect exposure.

Sector exposure should be documented before interpreting market evidence.

## Market Evidence

Market evidence asks what observed historical event-window data exists.

Use:

- raw historical returns;
- benchmark returns;
- abnormal returns;
- data quality flags;
- event-window limitations;
- missing-data disclosures.

Market evidence should be treated as descriptive historical context only.

## Explicit Prohibitions

The framework must not be used to produce:

- forecasts;
- probabilities;
- expected returns;
- price targets;
- trading signals;
- investment advice;
- automated buy, sell, or hold conclusions.

## Analyst Output Standard

A current relevance note should say:

- what is historically similar;
- what is structurally similar;
- what policy mechanism matches;
- what sector exposure is plausible;
- what market evidence exists;
- what caveats limit interpretation.

It should not say what will happen next.
